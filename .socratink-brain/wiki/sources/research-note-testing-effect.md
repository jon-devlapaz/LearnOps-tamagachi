# Testing Effect Research Note

## Summary

This source is a 2026-04-17 deep research note on the testing effect (a.k.a. retrieval practice effect), compiled from primary cognitive psychology literature including Roediger & Karpicke (2006), Rowland (2014, k=159 meta-analysis, g≈0.50), Carpenter (2009), the Karpicke episodic-context account, and the Van Gog/Sweller vs. Karpicke complex-materials debate.

The note establishes that the testing effect is one of the best-replicated findings in cognitive psychology and is broadly aligned with Socratink's "Generation Before Recognition" doctrine. The strongest, most actionable boundary condition is **feedback after failed retrieval** — Rowland 2014 found feedback raises effect size from g≈0.39 to g≈0.73. Without feedback, the effect collapses on failed attempts.

The note documents three Socratink-specific risks: (a) format mismatch between conversational free-recall and other criterion test formats limits transfer claims; (b) high element-interactivity in cluster drilling may attenuate the effect per the Van Gog/Sweller critique; (c) abandoned cold attempts without follow-up study are strictly worse than restudy. It proposes within-learner instrumentation — cold-attempt-and-study vs. study-only nodes, retention probes at 7 days — as the only way to prove the effect is operating in our app rather than relying on the literature alone.

The note also surfaces an open A/B-worthy question: Butler & Roediger (2008) found *delayed* feedback often beats *immediate* feedback for long-term retention. Socratink's immediate Socratic feedback is empirically reasonable but not optimized.
