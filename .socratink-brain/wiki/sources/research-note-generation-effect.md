# Generation Effect Research Note

## Summary

This source is a 2026-04-17 deep research note on the generation effect — the foundational citation set for Socratink's binding "Generation Before Recognition" doctrine. The note is anchored in Slamecka & Graf (1978, the founding paradigm) and Bertsch et al. (2007) meta-analysis (86 studies / 445 effect sizes, average d ≈ 0.40, growing to d ≈ 0.64 at >1-day delays — the effect *strengthens* at the retention horizons that matter for durable learning).

The note establishes the critical distinction between the testing effect (retrieval *after* study) and the generation effect (production *before or in lieu of* study). The pretesting effect (Kornell, Hays & Bjork 2009; Richland, Kornell & Kao 2009) is the special case where generation precedes instruction on novel material — directly relevant to Socratink's cold-attempt-first design.

The note surfaces two non-negotiable boundary conditions that Socratink must respect: (1) generation collapses without prior schema OR immediate corrective exposure (McElroy & Slamecka 1982 nonwords null; Hays-Kornell-Bjork 2013 unrelated-pairs null); (2) errorful generation only beats studying when feedback follows (Potts & Shanks 2014, 2019) — without feedback, errors can intrude. The cognitive-load critique from Kalyuga/Sweller (expertise reversal) is the strongest counterweight: for true novices on complex material, worked-examples-first may be more efficient than cold-attempt-first.

The note's most actionable recommendation: scoring a cold attempt is contraindicated by the literature, but **confidence** is the variable worth instrumenting (Metcalfe hypercorrection — high-confidence errors get corrected more reliably than low-confidence ones following feedback). It also surfaces a design question: should learners with zero prior schema get a structured "what do you already know / expect" prompt rather than a high-stakes recall demand?

The note flags a load-bearing uncertainty: the generation effect literature is primarily on word-pair and short-passage paradigms. Generalization to multi-concept Socratic dialogue is plausible but not directly tested. Socratink's own re-drill data is the missing primary evidence.
